Start here

Think of the AI as a brilliant new associate

One mental model carries this whole guide. Picture the AI as a brilliant, fast, eager-to-please new associate — with three quirks you have to manage.

This associate is fast and capable, but they take everything literally, they can't read your mind, and — most dangerous — they would rather confidently make something up than admit they don't know.

Everything that follows is just good delegation. A strong prompt is a well-written assignment: it says what you want, gives the background the associate couldn't know, and sets up a way to check the work. A weak prompt is the one-line hallway request that sends a junior associate down the wrong path for three hours.

Try it — predict, then reveal

A partner walks by and says: "See if we can get out of the Acme lease." A brilliant-but-literal new associate can't act on that yet. What does the request leave out?

At least: which jurisdiction's law governs; the actual lease terms (the associate hasn't read it); the goal (terminate? renegotiate? sublet? limit damages?); the deliverable (a quick read, a memo, an email to the client?); and any deadline. A good AI prompt front-loads exactly these — because the model, like the associate, won't ask.

Unlearn the old tricks

What changed: the model got smarter than the tricks

A lot of prompting folklore comes from 2023. Today's reasoning models (the current top-tier models) already work through problems step by step internally. Telling one to "think step by step" now duplicates what it already does and can make the answer worse.1 Give it the task and the constraints, then get out of the way.

Some habits still earn their keep; others are cargo cult. Sort them.

Sort each move — select it, then choose a bin

Why the "skip" pile: role-play openers and tip/threat tricks were never reliable, and explicit step-by-step instructions are redundant on models that already reason. Save chain-of-thought for cheaper, non-reasoning models, where it can still help.1

The durable core

The five jobs of a good prompt

Strip away the trends and a good prompt does five things. These hold across tools and model generations.

Walk through the five jobs

  1. 1 · Task

    Say exactly what you want done, and the goal behind it. "Summarize," "compare," "draft," "find authority for" — and what the output is for. Vague verbs get vague answers.

  2. 2 · Context

    Supply what the model can't know: jurisdiction, the operative facts, the audience, the constraints. Put the critical instructions first and last — models reliably lose information buried in the middle of a long prompt.1

  3. 3 · Format

    Name the shape of the answer: a memo, an IRAC paragraph, a three-column table, a bulleted issues list, a word limit. If you don't specify, you take what you get.

  4. 4 · Examples (when form matters)

    If the style or structure matters, show one short example of a good answer. One well-chosen example often beats a paragraph of instructions.

  5. 5 · Set up verification

    Ask it to cite its sources, flag uncertainty, and list the assumptions it made. You're not trusting the output — you're making it checkable. This sets up Section 6.

Weak prompt

find cases about emotional support animals and apartments

Know which tool you're holding

Open chatbot vs. grounded legal tool

This distinction matters more than any prompting trick.

An open chatbot (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini on their own) generates text from patterns in its training. It has no live connection to a legal database. Ask it for cases and it will produce something that looks like authority — sometimes real, sometimes invented.

A grounded legal tool (Lexis+ AI / Protégé, Westlaw's CoCounsel and AI-Assisted Research, vLex's Vincent) wraps a model around a curated legal database. It retrieves actual authority and answers from it, then links you to the source. You can ask in plain English. Grounding makes hallucinated citations far less likely — but, as Section 6 shows, not impossible.

Explain it in your own words

Because an open chatbot composes a plausible-sounding answer from training patterns rather than retrieving real authority. Asked for supportive cases, it will generate citations that look right but may not exist, or may not say what it claims. A grounded tool pulls from an actual legal database and links the source — so you can check it. Either way, you verify; with the open chatbot, verification is the only thing standing between you and a fake citation.

The legal-platform layer

Inside the legal research platforms

Grounded tools reward a slightly different style than a chatbot: focused legal questions with the material facts. Specifics below are current as of mid-2026 — features change fast, so confirm against each vendor's own guidance.

Platform details as of June 2026 — verify against current vendor docs

Westlaw

CoCounsel & AI-Assisted Research

  • Write a focused legal question; include the material facts, omit background.3
  • Don't prompt like a Boolean search — avoid "find cases that…" and proper names of parties or places.3
  • Treat it as a research accelerant, not a replacement for reading the authority.
LexisNexis

Lexis+ AI / Protégé

  • Ask gives a grounded answer to a legal question, tied to primary sources.2
  • Guided research proposes a step-by-step plan you can confirm, edit, or discard.2
  • It expects legal questions — not open-ended chit-chat.
vLex

Vincent

  • Fans one query into several searches across cases, statutes, and secondary sources, then reports back.4
  • Hyperlinks every authority to the quoted passage, so you can click straight to the source.4
  • States when it can't find real support instead of inventing it.4

The non-negotiable

Verify everything — even the grounded tools

Remember the associate who would rather invent than admit doubt. Grounding reduces that, but does not remove it. A Stanford study of purpose-built legal AI tools still found incorrect output more than a third of the time for one major research product, and roughly one in six for another.5

17%+
Lexis+ AI responses with incorrect information (Stanford, 2024–25)5
34%+
Westlaw AI-Assisted Research, same study5
1,300+
Court filings with AI-fabricated content catalogued by mid-20266

The consequences are real: courts have imposed escalating sanctions, suspensions, and bar referrals on lawyers who filed AI-invented citations.7 The duty of candor and competence doesn't bend because a machine drafted the cite.

The verification standard for any AI-supplied authority: confirm the source exists, confirm it actually says what it's cited for, and check that nothing better was missed. And never paste confidential or privileged material into a tool your institution hasn't approved.
