# Instructor Framework: Organizing-Structure Intake

*Consumed by SKILL.md Phase 0 (organizing-structure interview) and Phase 1 (learning architecture).*
*For the pedagogy rationale behind every rule here, see [`pedagogy.md`](pedagogy.md) — especially Rule 1.*

---

## Why structure matters

Every effective explainer has one coherent throughline — a single organizing idea that tells a learner where they are, where they're going, and why the sequence makes sense. Without it, sections feel like a list of facts. With the wrong one imposed on the content, learners feel the seams: the framework doesn't quite fit, and the effort of tracking it competes with learning the actual material.

The skill's job in Phase 0 is to find the structure that is already latent in how the instructor thinks about the topic — and surface it, not invent one.

---

## The three kinds of organizing structure

### (a) Process framework

**What it is:** A replicable, step-by-step procedure the learner will actually perform. The steps have a meaningful order. Skipping or reversing them causes a worse outcome, not just a different one. The whole point of teaching the topic is to teach the skill of executing the process.

**Hallmarks:**
- The instructor naturally says "first you do X, then Y, then Z."
- There is a right answer to "what comes next?"
- The framework has a name the instructor uses — or could name — when teaching.
- Learners who master the framework can apply it to novel problems.

**When it fits:** Procedural skills. Legal research processes (Watson/Drake's four-phase process; Boolean vs. natural-language search decision; source-validation workflow). AI prompting frameworks. Statutory analysis workflows. Citation-checking protocols. Document-review procedures.

**When it does not fit:** Doctrinal topics where the instructor's goal is understanding a concept, not performing a procedure. Even if a topic *could* be shoehorned into a pseudo-process ("Step 1: Identify the issue, Step 2: Find the rule…"), do not do that unless the instructor genuinely teaches a named, replicable process for approaching the doctrine.

---

### (b) Conceptual mental model

**What it is:** A way of thinking about how something works — relationships, distinctions, categories, or a structure internal to the concept itself — that has no step-by-step order but gives the learner a durable frame for understanding and applying the concept.

**Hallmarks:**
- The instructor's goal is that learners *understand* something, not that they *do* something in a fixed sequence.
- The concept has meaningful internal structure: parts and their relationships, a taxonomy, contrasting categories, a causal mechanism, a historical logic.
- The instructor reaches for analogies, diagrams, or tables when explaining it.
- There is no single "first step" — different entry points into the material are equally valid.

**When it fits:** Doctrinal law topics (hearsay and its exceptions; the elements and policy of strict liability; the rule against perpetuities; First Amendment doctrine). Constitutional structure. Legal history. Statutory interpretation canons as a taxonomy. Any topic where the instructor's goal is comprehension and application of a framework of ideas, not execution of a procedure.

**When it does not fit:** Topics where the instructor actually does have a replicable process they want students to internalize. The fact that a topic involves "thinking carefully" does not make it a conceptual mental model — the question is whether the instructor has a named, teachable structure for how to approach it.

---

### (c) Plain logical sequence

**What it is:** A straightforward ordering of related material with no deeper structure — neither a procedure nor a conceptual model. Items have an order because one depends on another, or because the instructor customarily teaches them this way, but there is no framework the learner needs to internalize.

**Hallmarks:**
- The content is primarily informational: background, context, vocabulary, rules to know.
- The instructor would say "we cover X before Y because you need X to understand Y" — not because Y is a step after X in a process.
- There is no framework the student needs to remember and apply independently.

**When it fits:** Orientation material. Vocabulary-heavy introductions. Surveys that precede a process or model. Topics where the instructor explicitly says "this is just context — there's no particular structure to it."

**When it does not fit:** Any topic where the instructor has a genuine process or conceptual structure, even an informal one. A plain sequence is the fallback, not the default — prefer surfacing latent structure over flattening it.

## Phase-0 elicitation: questions to ask the instructor

The goal is not to run through a checklist but to hear how the instructor naturally describes the topic. Listen for signals that reveal which kind of structure is already present.

**Opening:** "Tell me about this topic the way you'd explain it to students at the start of class — what do they need to walk away able to do or understand?"

**If the answer sounds procedural** ("they need to be able to run a search, evaluate sources, then…"):
- "Is there a specific sequence or framework you use — something you'd put on a slide or whiteboard?"
- "Does that sequence have a name you use with students, or steps they're expected to memorize and apply?"
- "If a student skipped step two and went straight to step three, would they get a worse result, or just a different one?"
- "When you give students a research problem, what do you want to see them do *first*?"

**If the answer sounds conceptual** ("they need to understand how X works, why the rule developed this way…"):
- "What are the key distinctions or categories in this topic that students most often confuse?"
- "If you were drawing this on a whiteboard, what would it look like — a diagram, a table, a list of contrasts?"
- "Is there a mental model or way of thinking about this that you find yourself coming back to when explaining it?"
- "What does a student who really *gets* this topic understand that a student who only *knows the rule* doesn't?"

**If the structure is unclear:**
- "When you teach this topic, does it have a beginning-middle-end, or is it more like a map of related ideas?"
- "Is there something students need to be able to do after this, or is the goal more that they understand it?"
- "Have you seen another teacher or a textbook organize this well? What did they do?"
- "If I asked you to name the three or four most important things about this topic, in order — what would they be, and why that order?"

**Checking fit of a candidate structure:**
- "Does this match how you actually think about teaching this, or does it feel like I'm forcing a shape onto it?"
- "If a student used this structure on an exam or in practice, would it help them or get in their way?"

## Deciding which kind fits

Work through this in order.

**1. Does the instructor have an explicit process framework?**

An explicit process framework means: the instructor uses a named or nameable procedure (Watson/Drake; the IRAC sequence for a specific skill; an AI-prompting framework with labeled components). It has steps the instructor expects students to internalize and apply. If yes — and if this is how the instructor wants to teach the skill — use it as the organizing structure. Confirm: "Is this the framework you want students to walk away able to execute?"

**2. If no explicit framework: does a latent process exist?**

Sometimes an instructor describes a process informally without naming it. If they consistently say "first… then… finally…" with the same sequence, and the steps represent a learner action (not just a logical connection), a process framework may be latent. Surface it: "It sounds like there's a sequence here — would it help to name the steps?" If the instructor confirms, co-develop it (see below). If they say "not really — it depends on the situation," do not force one.

**3. Is the topic a doctrinal or conceptual one with no meaningful procedure?**

This is the most common case for law and many other disciplines. The instructor's goal is that students understand a body of doctrine, a concept, or an analytical framework — not that they execute steps. Use a conceptual mental model. Find the structure that is internal to the concept: its key categories, the relationships among them, the distinctions that organize the doctrine. This is not a consolation prize for topics without a process; it is the right structure for these topics.

**4. Is there no structure at all?**

Only fall back to a plain logical sequence if the instructor confirms there is no meaningful structure — the content is genuinely context or vocabulary before something else. This is unusual. Most topics have more internal structure than they appear to at first.

**In every case, confirm the fit with the instructor before proceeding to Phase 1.** The structure belongs to the instructor's teaching, not to the skill's template.

## The "never force it" rule

A forced organizing structure is actively harmful. It adds cognitive overhead (the learner tracks the framework on top of the content), creates seams where the content doesn't quite fit the frame, and signals to the learner that the structure is the point — when the content is the point.

This applies in two directions:

**Do not impose a process framework on content that has none.** If a doctrinal topic has no genuine step-by-step procedure the instructor teaches, do not invent a pseudo-process ("Step 1: Identify the issue"). The content has its own structure; find that instead.

**Do not impose a process framework that isn't how the instructor teaches the skill.** Even when a process framework exists in the domain (Watson/Drake for legal research; a known AI-prompting structure), it is the wrong structure if the instructor doesn't use it, doesn't teach it, or wants to teach a different one. The organizing structure must be *the instructor's* — not the skill's idea of how the subject works.

The pedagogy basis: Rule 1 of the enforced spine in [`pedagogy.md`](pedagogy.md) — the Coherence principle shows that imposing structure that doesn't match the content's own logic adds load rather than removing it. A leaky framework is worse than an honest sequence.

When uncertain whether a structure fits, choose the simpler option. An honest plain sequence that follows the logic of the content is better than a process framework with steps that don't quite apply to everything.

## How structure maps to section order

Once the kind of structure is established, it directly determines how sections are ordered in Phase 1.

**Process framework → sections follow the steps.** Each major step of the process is one or more sections. Introductory material (context, why the process matters) precedes step one. Optional: a synthesis section after the final step showing how the whole process connects. The learner can track their position in the process at every point in the explainer.

**Conceptual mental model → sections follow the concept's own logic.** Order by:
- *Dependency:* concepts the learner must understand first come before concepts that build on them.
- *Contrast:* if the concept is organized by key distinctions, introduce the distinction before the subcategories.
- *Salience:* the most important or most often misunderstood aspect comes early, not buried.
Do not try to create a false "first… then…" sequence. Sections may be labeled by the concept's own categories ("The three elements," "When the rule applies; when it doesn't") rather than by numbered steps.

**Plain logical sequence → sections follow content dependency.** What must be known to understand the next thing comes first. Avoid reverse-engineering a structure where none exists.

In every case: the section order should feel natural to the instructor when they read it. If it doesn't, the structure choice is wrong.

## Co-developing a structure when the instructor has none

Sometimes an instructor knows their material deeply but has never articulated how they organize it. This is common for experienced teachers who improvise successfully but haven't made their implicit structure explicit. The co-development process:

**1. Elicit the components, then sort them.**
Ask: "What are the three or four most important ideas in this topic?" Write them down without imposing order. Then: "If you were introducing these to a student who knew nothing, which would you explain first? Why?"

**2. Look for the latent logic.**
As the instructor explains the ordering, listen for what kind of logic is driving it:
- "Because you need to know X before Y makes sense" → dependency logic → use it as the order.
- "Because the first step in the process is X" → process framework → name and confirm the steps.
- "Because X is the central idea and the rest hang off it" → hub-and-spoke mental model → put X first, use it as the organizing concept.

**3. Reflect the structure back.**
Draft a one-sentence summary: "It sounds like the organizing structure is [name or description]. The sections would go [A → B → C] because [reason]. Does that match how you think about teaching this?" The instructor should be able to confirm or correct it without qualification. If they say "sort of, but…" — keep going until the structure is unambiguous.

**4. Name it.**
Give the structure a name, even a provisional one. This helps the instructor recognize it in the draft and helps Phase 1 anchor the section order.

**5. If nothing emerges, say so.**
If after genuine effort no structure surfaces — the instructor says "it's really just a collection of things to know" — use a plain logical sequence and note in the build that this topic has no deeper organizing structure. Do not invent one.

## Worked example 1: process framework (legal research)

**Topic:** Running a research project on an unfamiliar area of law.

**Interview signals:** The instructor uses "first you need to…" throughout. They reference a specific framework by name (Watson/Drake). They grade research memos partly on whether students followed a defensible research path. When asked "what does a student do first?", they answer immediately and consistently.

**Decision:** Process framework. The instructor teaches a named, replicable procedure. Students are expected to internalize it and apply it to novel research problems.

**Structure elicited:**
1. Assess the problem — identify what kind of legal question it is and what sources are likely relevant before touching a database.
2. Build a search plan — identify search terms, Boolean variants, relevant jurisdictions and date ranges.
3. Execute and evaluate sources — run searches, retrieve materials, assess authority and currency.
4. Synthesize and validate — pull together what you found, check that it's still good law, identify gaps.

**Section order:** Follows the four steps. An orientation section before Step 1 explains why having a framework matters (not just diving into a search bar). Each step section includes at least one Constructive interaction: Step 2 has a classify-and-rank exercise on search terms; Step 3 has a predict-then-reveal on source authority; a retrieval check falls after Step 2 and another after Step 4.

**What was rejected:** A conceptual mental model organized around "types of legal sources" was considered (primary/secondary; mandatory/persuasive; etc.). That structure exists in the domain but is not how this instructor teaches this skill — their goal is the research process, not source taxonomy as an end in itself.

## Worked example 2: doctrinal topic, no step-by-step process (hearsay)

**Topic:** Hearsay — the rule, its rationale, and the major exceptions.

**Interview signals:** The instructor describes hearsay as a "doctrine you have to feel your way through" rather than a checklist. When asked "what's the first step?", they say "well, it depends on what the statement is being offered for." They reach for examples and diagrams when explaining it. Their goal is that students can spot a hearsay issue, apply the exceptions correctly, and understand why the rule exists — not that they execute a fixed procedure.

**Decision:** Conceptual mental model. There is no replicable process the instructor teaches; the goal is understanding. The concept has significant internal structure (the definition, the offered-for distinction, the exceptions and their rationale), but that structure is relational and categorical, not procedural.

**Structure elicited:**
- The core concept: what hearsay *is* (an out-of-court statement offered for the truth of the matter asserted) and what it *isn't* (the "not for the truth" distinction — the most common source of confusion).
- Why the rule exists: the rationale (untested credibility, confrontation value), which explains why the exceptions make sense.
- The exceptions as a taxonomy: grouped by their underlying rationale (necessity + reliability, circumstantial guarantees of trustworthiness, party admissions), so the student can reason about unfamiliar situations rather than just memorizing a list.
- A synthesis: how to work through a hearsay problem — not a step-by-step process, but a sense of what questions to ask.

**Section order:** (1) The definition and the "not for the truth" distinction first — this is the conceptual prerequisite for everything else. (2) The rationale — why the rule exists, which is needed to make sense of the exceptions. (3) The exceptions, grouped by their rationale, not listed alphabetically. (4) Synthesis: working through a hearsay problem.

A predict-then-reveal in section 1 asks learners to classify examples (hearsay or not, and why). A retrieval check after section 3 tests exception application. A second retrieval check at the end tests the full analysis. A self-explain asks learners to articulate why a specific exception makes sense given the rationale.

**What was rejected:** A pseudo-process framework ("Step 1: Is it a statement? Step 2: Out of court? Step 3: Offered for the truth?...") was explicitly declined. The IRAC flowchart approach exists in many study aids but is not how this instructor teaches the doctrine — the instructor wants students to understand the concept, not run a decision tree. A decision tree applied to hearsay without understanding the rationale produces mechanical answers that fall apart on novel facts. The conceptual mental model serves the instructor's actual goal.
